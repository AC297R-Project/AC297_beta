Conclusion

Experimentation:

We were able to reduce correlation and volatility over traditional S&P hedging in our dynamic portfolio Greedy implementation, but the method returned results that were lower than holding money in savings accounts would have been in that time.  While we did not ultimately identify a risk management strategy that we believe would be practical to apply in real life, we did perform careful, thorough, and systematic experiments that allowed us to confidently conclude that this beta hedging market optimization is not a promising direction. 

The following list summarizes the experimentation strategies that we used to reach this conclusion:

1) Runs in different market conditions.  We trained and tested our method in a variety of real-life market conditions, including during bull markets, volatile markets, free-falling crashes, and early recoveries.  While we did see that we could consistently mitigate losses during crashes, we always saw that the lost returns during good times (which are much more common) significantly outweighed the prevented losses. 

2) A variety of portfolios.  Within each time period that we tested, we trained and tested on many portfolios to rule out that we were overfitting to a particular portfolio.  We were able to generate measures of variability (visualized as error bars or bands in our plots) in our results in this way, and saw that this level of variability somewhat higher for our optimized hedged portfolios than it was for S&P hedged portfolios, and unhedged portfolios.  This allowed us to conclude that while we might improve the measures of interest (correlation and volatility) on average, the inconsistency of these results stemming from the stochasticity in our optimization may dampen the benefits of optimizing for stability. 

3) Many repetitions on a single portfolio.  We also performed tests in which we performed the same optimization to a single portfolio multiple times.  In this way, we were able to observe the variability in results that came solely from the optimization.  While this variability was smaller than the variability that came from the variety in portfolios in our batch simulations described above, it was useful to observe that each run of the optimization did not guarantee the same results, and this would be important to a user who might be considering using this method instead of traditional S&P hedging. 

4) Long term runs with dynamic portfolios.  To observe the long-term performance of our method, we finally brought together all of the forms of experimentation described above into a single large batch experiment, in which we simulated investing continuously over a period of 10 years, updating the portfolio every 3 months, and performed the optimization 100 times for the same portfolio in each quarter.  This allowed us to generalize the patterns that we were seeing in our shorter-term simulations to a long time horizon, and conclusively say that for a medium investment horizon, the reduction in correlation and volatility that comes from our method is probably not worth the loss in long-term returns. 

Other hedging strategies that would be interesting to pursue:

1) Delta hedging: Hedge the risk associated with price movements in the underlying asset by offsetting long and short positions. You make money based on volatility, not returns.

2) Extension to beta hedging (using options that are cheaper): Buying only index put options to hedge the value of a portfolio.

3) Hedge directly against market volatility by trading VIX.

4) Long-short portfolio.

5) Stay in cash.


Discussions:

1) Is our greedy portfolio selection strategy actually effective?

2) Is beta hedging ever the right choice?

3) How would transaction costs affect things?

4) Could beta hedging still work while not changing the entire hedging market every quarter?
